Rethinking NYC’s Buses

A Four-Part Investigation

The Future of MTA Buses

New York’s buses carry about a million riders each day, linking neighborhoods that subways often miss. Yet the system has grown slower, less trusted, and more costly over the years. This series examines the data, the budgets, and the politics—and explores what it would take to make the network faster, fairer, and more reliable.

01

Crisis by the Numbers

Ridership, fare evasion and the nation’s slowest buses: why the system is faltering, and what the data really says.

Data • Reliability • Performance
02

The Business of Buses

An examination of the MTA’s finances and efficiency—how effectively subsidies and fares are translated into service, viewed through revenue, operating costs, and capital spending.

Finance • Efficiency
03

Rewiring the Bus

A blueprint for change: what the MTA could improve, how to fund it, and where to trim costs. Drawing lessons from other cities, examining union dynamics, rapid-transit lanes, fare pilots, and even bold moves like eliminating free parking, this section surveys fixes both practical and political.

Policy • Reform • Finance
04

Transit and Equity

Bus failures fall hardest on working-class riders. This section explores who benefits, who is left behind, and how targeted improvements, cost-effective reforms, and smarter funding could make the system fairer, faster, and more reliable across the city.

Equity • Community • Social Impact

Research Methodology

This series draws on MTA ridership data, internal budgets, city planning documents, and interviews with transit experts and riders. Findings are cross-checked with academic research and global case studies.
