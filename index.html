Ridership

Bus ridership in New York has fallen sharply over the past two decades, from nearly 2 million daily riders in 2002 to about 1.1 million today. Annual trips totaled just over 409 million in 2024, still far below pre-pandemic levels, when paid ridership was more than 40 percent higher. The pandemic dealt the hardest blow, with usage dropping to 255 million trips in 2020, and recovery has been gradual since. McKinsey projections for MTA bus ridership have consistently overestimated the pace of this recovery.

Who rides matters as much as how many. Bus riders are disproportionately low-income, immigrant, and essential workers: the average household income is under $30,000, more than half of riders are immigrants, and 40 percent work in frontline roles. Buses also carry a greater share of disabled New Yorkers compared with the subway, making them a lifeline for many who cannot easily switch modes.

Future growth depends on more than population trends. The MTA projects gradual ridership increases, reaching about three-quarters of pre-pandemic levels by 2026. Yet without more reliable service and faster travel times, even steady gains may not fully restore the systemâ€™s role as the affordable, accessible backbone of outer-borough mobility.
