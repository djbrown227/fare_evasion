Long-Term Decline

Bus ridership in New York City has been in steady decline for more than two decades. Daily riders have dropped from nearly 2 million in 2002 to just about 1.1 million in 2024 â€” a loss of roughly 45%. This persistent decline reflects lasting shifts in commuting patterns, competition from cars and ride-hailing, and service reliability issues.

Brooklyn continues to account for the highest bus ridership, while Staten Island carries the lowest. These borough-level differences highlight both the essential role buses play in certain communities and the uneven impact of ridership losses across the city.

Pandemic and Aftermath

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated these long-term declines. Despite partial recovery, ridership remains far below pre-2019 levels. Daily bus trips hover around 1.4 million, but commuting patterns have permanently shifted with the rise of remote work and reduced weekday demand.

Unlike other transit systems that rely more heavily on subsidies, the MTA depends significantly on farebox revenue. This means falling ridership translates directly into financial pressure. Even with projected fare and toll increases through 2027â€“28, pricing alone cannot close the gap â€” ridership losses are structural, not temporary. The challenge for the MTA is not simply restoring old patterns, but adapting service to meet new realities.
